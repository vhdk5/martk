14. Calculating: Competitor

A competitive analysis will help you answer important business questions including:

  • Who are our key competitors?
  • What are the objectives of our key competitors? How does that differ from our objective?
  • What are their strengths and weaknesses?
  • What threats do they pose?
  • What opportunities do they have?
  • What strategies are they pursuing and how would they affect us?
  • Based on their past action how are they likely to respond to changes in our business?

Once you’re ready to begin a competitive analysis, follow these steps:

Step 1: Create a list of your primary competitors

If you aren’t familiar with who your competitors are, you can take a look on the web using Google or Google Maps. You might also look at directories for any industry groups, or the vendor directory at trade shows you would attend.

The competitor worth evaluating will operate on a similar business objective, market to a similar audience that you’re marketing to, and sell similar products.

Keep your list focused to 5-7 as you want to be able to use this information in an actionable manner. A list that is too large will create too much noise.

Step 2: Create a spreadsheet

As you gather data on your competitors, organize it in a spreadsheet. You’re looking to capture initial insights on:

  • Price
  • Products
  • Social Media Accounts
  • Level of Social Engagement (Frequency, Last Publishing Date, Etc)
  • Content Used

Step 3: Identify primary/secondary competitors

Start by making a list of your competitors, and categorizing them into one of the following: Primary Competitors, Secondary Competitors, or Tertiary Competitors.

Primary competitors are targeting the same audience or have a similar product.

Secondary competitors are offering a higher or lower end version of your product, or selling something similar but to a completely different target market.

Tertiary competitors are any business that is related to yours. For instance, if you sell tennis rackets, a tertiary competitor may sell tennis balls. They’re not a threat, but could become one if they pivot.

Step 4: Identify your competitor’s market positioning.

Take a look at their website and any marketing messaging and ask the following:

  • What is their value proposition?
  • Why would their customer buy this? Is it Price? Experience?
  • What makes them different?
  • What features do they highlight in their marketing copy?
  • What makes them unique?

Step 5: Gather information

  • Sign up for their newsletter
  • Follow their blog
  • Setup Google Alerts for media mentions
  • Follow them on social media
  • Purchase a product
  • Review any of their sales collateral
  • Look through all their press releases
  • Contact their customer service team
  • Return or refund the product
  • Identify their pricing model

Step 6: Read reviews

Find as many reviews of your competitors as possible — read them, categorize them, and highlight what customers like and dislike.

Use these reviews to see if you can find a strength or weakness you can capitalize on.

There are several tools you might find helpful as you conduct your analysis:

SimilarWeb
SimilarWeb offers insights into estimated monthly visits and key traffic sources for a website.

Mailcharts
Mailcharts can give you insight into your competitors’ email marketing, from the frequency of email sends to their subject line tactics.

Buzzsumo
You can search your competitor’s domain in Buzzsumo to see their top-performing content.

Alexa
Alex helps you identify the audience demographics and search rankings related to a website.
