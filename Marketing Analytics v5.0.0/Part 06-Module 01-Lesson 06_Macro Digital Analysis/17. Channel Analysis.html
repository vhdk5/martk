17. Channel Analysis

There are a lot of channels that you can market in. Truthfully, you’re not going to have the time, resources, or budget to marketing in every channel simultaneously — and even if you did have the resources, you wouldn’t want to.

What we need to do, is evaluate the performance of each channel in relation to one another. You would do this at a granular level. For example:

You may be using television, magazines, newspapers, transit signs, billboards, celebrity endorsements and bulk mailing. You might even group sponsorships at trade shows or on podcasts. You may even be running Facebook Ads, Google Ads, and YouTube Ads.

Start by making a list of all the channels you’re presently using, and identify the goals set for each of those channels. Next, gather the metrics used to determine if that goal was met. From here, you’ll need to compare the performance between each channel. In some cases, you may simply evaluate ROI or ROS on each channel. Other channels may not be tied to a revenue goal, and for that you’ll have to evaluate the effort.

Gather the goals that were established for each channel, and identify whether that goal was met, and how significant that goal is to the bottom line of the business.

To compare these effectively, evaluate the effort required to maintain each channel, and the value you just outlined. From there, you can plot this on a value-matrix.

Plot where these channels land on this grid. If it’s easier, you can assign values (1-10) for effort, and value -- and actually plot the exact dot position on the axis.

With the complete matrix, it’s easier to decide which channels are worth investing in further. Start optimizing channels in the high value, low effort section.

Keep in mind, just because something is High Effort and Low Value doesn’t mean it’s not worth exploring. You’re simply scoring these in relation to one another, low value could still mean a significant return on investment, it’s just not as significant as something in the high value section.
