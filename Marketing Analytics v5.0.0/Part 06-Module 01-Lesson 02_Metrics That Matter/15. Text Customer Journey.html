15. Text: Customer Journey

When you know the customer journey, you’ll know where you are deploying solutions and how you are measuring those solutions. The funnel has helped us understand the steps they’ll go through — but there’s still a journey within those steps. A user can have many touch-points within each stage, so it’s important that we create a reference journey. The systematic journey that we ideally want the buyer to take which reflects on the idea we are pushing this buyer down our marketing funnel.

We understand the buyer will go through the stages from Awareness to Action and hopefully to Loyalty.

But, what does the buyer journey look like within those stages?

How did the buyer become aware? Did they walk into a store and see the product on the shelf? Did they hear about it from a friend? Did they click on a social media advertisement? If they saw an item on the shelf, were their competing products alongside yours? If so, what does that do for their journey? Will they now be comparing your product to the one next to it? And if so, are they comparing it on cost? On benefit?

The buyer may be moving up and down the funnel — there’s so many variables that we are working to account for.

Think about all the steps involved in the decision making process. Within each stage, the customer will define their own path, interact with various brand touch-points, and hopefully move towards the next step in the funnel.

With the increase of mobile devices and digital connectivity, consumers are exposed to too much of everything. They’ve got access to a lot of information, and they’re constantly being bombarded with messaging up & down the funnel from a plethora of brands.

It’s important that you map the consumer journey and identify where consumers will cross into your funnel.

Take catalog of how a buyer navigates a funnel. This is a rather robust undertaking, and it’s often best handled by treating it in an agile manner. You must iterate on your buyer journey’s — you cannot conceivably map out every journey, every touchpoint, every interaction.

So, start by creating the current buyer journey for these two groups:

  • Group A: The buyer who is aware of their problem, not ready to purchase, and not aware of your solution.

  • Group B: The buyer who is aware of their problem, and ready to purchase.

What does that buyer journey look like for Group A and Group B.

  • What steps would they take?
  • How would they go through a funnel?

Look at the funnel and visualize the buyer becoming aware, becoming interested, and so on.

You can do this for your company, or any brand that you follow.

Ask yourself:

  • How do they become aware of their problem?
  • How would they become aware of a solution?
  • How would they become interested in a brand?
  • Why would they desire a particular brand?
  • What would motivate them to convert?
  • How do you move them along in this funnel ?

Start to develop this into a map, as you saw in the video.

You’ll want to outline all the ways they interact with your brand — they come into your website, visit your store, read your social media posts, receive an e-mail, etc.

This thought experiment, that will help you begin to connect the dots between your consumer and the analytics you are measuring.
