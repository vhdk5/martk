07. Text: The Analytic Brief

The format we use for the Analytic Brief is an adaptation from Cesar A Brea’s book Pragmalytics.

Cesar’s approach is to bridge the gap between marketing campaign plans and how we measure and evaluate their performance.

In today’s marketing landscape, we have to be agile — essentially, more flexible, as we launch campaigns. We’re constantly testing and improving, which means we have to adapt how we measure. As we roll out new campaigns, we need to be ready to evaluate their performance, and we need to be sure that we truly understand what our objectives are and how our customer will come to interact with the areas of our business that guide them towards those objectives.

To keep things organized, and prevent important metrics from slipping through the cracks, we need a framework. This is where the Analytic Brief fits in.

The analytic brief outlines the various aspects of the business and does so in a way that is clear and concise so that others can understand the information as its being presented.

When preparing the brief it’s important that you consider the following:

Keep it Simple

There’s no need to go into extreme depth, or complicate the brief. Simply use the information that you have available and outline things as best you can. The brief creates a natural opportunity to ask questions to clarify what you’re measuring, and those conversations alone provide tremendous value.

Don’t make it a monumental task

Briefs tend to feel like burdensome projects, but the Analytic Brief is meant to be quick. You should be able to create an in-depth brief in half-a-day, and that includes the time to sit down with stakeholders if the project is beyond your team.

Fill it out

Don’t leave areas of the brief blank — the whole intent is to help you capture all the important areas of your business and the customer journey. If you find gaps, solve them. Don’t skip sections, or leave areas blank assuming you will solve for it later. The entire goal is to figure out everything ahead of time, so you don’t run into problems later.

Attached to the brief is the documentation on your customer journey. Use this visual map to further evaluate all the metrics you will be capturing, and evaluate whether the customer journey has revealed any gaps in the brief itself.

The analytic brief is a valuable framework, and it’s worth exploring further because it will help create actionability out of your metrics.
