14. Key Terms

Key Terms

Term Definition
Linear scale A range of values where the value between two consecutive points on the line does not change no matter how high or low you are on the scale. It shows the absolute values.
Logarithmic scale A range of values where the value between two consecutive points not only changes, but also has a distinct pattern. This shows the rate of change over time.

There are two main reasons to use logarithmic scales in charts and graphs. The first is to respond to skewness towards large values; i.e., cases in which one or a few points are much larger than the bulk of the data. The second is to show percent change or multiplicative factors.

In the example, the linear scale graph shows the absolute number of users by default channel grouping over time. The large value dwarfs the smaller values, in other words, users from organic search dwarf users from paid search and affiliates.

When you switch from the linear scale graph to the logarithmic scale graph, the logarithmic scale graph shows the rate of change of the number of users by default channel group over time. Each tick mark on the y axis is the previous tick mark multiplied by 10. On a logarithmic scale, the distance between each power of 10 is the same. So the distance from 1 to 10 on the graph is the same as the distance from 10 to 100, which is the same as the distance from 100 to 1000.

The logarithmic graph makes the rate of change of users from each default channel group clear in the visualization. The values themselves don’t change, they are displayed on a different scale.
